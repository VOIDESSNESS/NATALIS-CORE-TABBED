TFR TODAY
In 1980, China made the most consequential demographic decision in human history. To control what the state considered an unmanageable population, it limited most couples to a single child — backed by fines, forced sterilisations, and late-term abortions carried out by government officials.

The policy worked. Perhaps too well. China's fertility rate collapsed — and then kept collapsing long after the policy was lifted in 2015. The cultural transformation was irreversible. A generation raised without siblings does not suddenly want three children because the law changed.

Today China's TFR sits at approximately 1.09 — half of replacement. Its population is aging faster than any economy can adapt to. The decisions made in 1980 are now the bill that comes due in 2040.
Japan: The Demographic Twilight
1970 — PRESENT
▼
Japan's TFR crossed below 2.1 in 1974 and never returned. Today at 1.20, Japan has more people over 65 (29%) than under 15 (11.4%). It sells more adult incontinence products than baby diapers. Its government has spent over $20B in pronatalist policies with negligible effect. The reason: the cost of raising a child in Tokyo's competitive education environment is catastrophic relative to average income, and the cultural pressure of "keeping up" has made childbearing feel financially suicidal for most young Japanese couples.

By 2050, Japan's population is projected to fall from 124M to under 100M. Its Self-Defense Force is already struggling with recruitment. By 2070, the structural economic drag from an inverted age pyramid may be irreversible without radical immigration policy — which Japan, culturally, continues to resist.
South Korea: The End Point
2000 — PRESENT
▼
South Korea's TFR of 0.72 in 2023 is not just a record — it is a signal of total systemic failure. The country has the world's highest private education spending per capita, one of the most brutal corporate work cultures, and housing costs in Seoul that make London look affordable.

Young Koreans have coined terms for their situation: sampo sedae (giving up on dating, marriage, and children) and opo sedae (adding jobs and home ownership to the list of things abandoned). The government has allocated $200B since 2006 to reverse the trend. The TFR has continued to fall every year.

At 0.72, South Korea's population will halve in roughly 40 years even with optimistic immigration. Its military — which relies on conscription — already faces a manpower crisis. This is what demographic endgame looks like in practice.
Russia's Night Strategy & Its Failure
2006 — PRESENT
▼
Russia's TFR hit a historic low of 1.16 in 1999 following the post-Soviet economic collapse. The state responded with cash bonuses for second children ("maternity capital"), national "Day of Conception" holidays — and at various points, rhetoric about cutting nighttime internet and power to encourage "productive" time at home.

The TFR briefly rose to 1.78 in 2015. Then fell again. The war in Ukraine has since accelerated demographic damage: estimated 100,000–300,000 young men killed or permanently disabled, hundreds of thousands fled the country. Russia's current TFR sits around 1.42.

The lesson is brutal: you cannot force births when the structural economic cost of having children remains unchanged. Switching off the lights is a metaphor, not a solution.
Africa: The Other Crisis
NOW — 2100
▼
Niger's TFR of 6.73 is the world's highest. Africa overall sits around 4.2. By 2100, Africa's population is projected to reach 3.9 billion — more than the entire world in 1970. This is not a straightforward win. High TFR in low-resource environments creates a different kind of crisis: youth unemployment, political instability, accelerated resource depletion, and mass migration pressure on lower-TFR nations.

The relationship between TFR and development is well-established: as education, female workforce participation, and urbanisation increase — TFR falls. Africa is on that path. The question is whether the transition happens fast enough to avoid the worst of both extremes.
The Economic Trap: Why No Incentive Works
SYSTEMIC ANALYSIS
▼
The global economic structure is, by design, hostile to parenthood. Wages have not kept pace with the cost of housing, childcare, healthcare, or education in any developed economy since the 1980s. The real cost of a child — not just money, but opportunity, career trajectory, freedom, and mental health — is so extreme that no government cash payment comes close to offsetting it.

This is not a failure of individual choice. It is the rational output of a system that was never built to support reproduction at scale in high-cost environments.

The people who control the parameters of that system — capital, policy, and media — benefit from a large, anxious, mobile, and relatively rootless labour pool. Stable family formation, community cohesion, and generational wealth accumulation reduce corporate leverage over workers. Whether this is coordinated or emergent is irrelevant. The effect is the same.

Until the foundational cost structure changes — housing, education, healthcare, time — no surface-level incentive will move the needle beyond statistical noise.
